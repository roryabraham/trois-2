INTEGRATED IDENTITY

Trois 2 is representative of our individual identities combined into a single, collective, us. Trois (pronounced tʁwa) means “three” in French, and is meant to symbolize the diversity and continuity of our powerful and equitable trinity. Trois, on its own, however, fails to describe the multifarious abilities and dynamic characteristics that have shaped our group’s identity. Our brand identity could perhaps be better described by: “Out of three, two!” Out of three members, we have two males, two California residents, and two people pursuing a degree in Engineering. Three remain in our group after two dispersed. At our shared intellectual experience, our final group of three is represented in two separate photos. While there are many pairs of similarities and differences, the three of us are bound by a collective identity. We share a passion to integrate the knowledge and skills we will develop in our STEM disciplines into innovative solutions for the urgent struggles that face humanity in the ever-changing “unscripted problems of tomorrow.” We share a drive to inspire, to meet our collective future with the intention of making “change that works toward the dignity of all.” We are Changemakers. We are Trois 2.

Gallery:

Over the course of the semester, we have assembled into groups to take photographs, documenting significant moments throughout our collective educational journey at USD. We have developed our close-reading skills to study photography and literature, learning to decipher a greater contextual and symbolic meaning from a snapshot representation of reality, be it textual or photographic. Inside each photograph, there is a whole world of knowledge, history, personality, and identity that can be observed, analyzed, and speculated on using the scrupulous lens of close-reading. Susan Sontag argues that humanity is, as it always has been, trapped within Plato’s cave, and that “Plato’s derogatory attitude toward images liken them to shadows---transitory, minimally informative, immaterial, impotent co-presences of the real things which cast them.” In asking key questions and drawing connections with concepts we have studied throughout the semester, our perception of these photographic representations of reality may become more accurate to the contextual, historical, emotional, and societal truth within the physical reality. In performing close-reading, we attempt, within the limits of our ability and knowledge and understanding, to climb out of Plato’s cave.
